About Diwali

Diwali, also known as Deepavali, is one of the most important festivals in India. It's a five-day celebration that marks the victory of light over darkness and good over evil.

The festival gets its name from the row (avali) of clay lamps (deepa) that Indians light outside their homes to symbolize the inner light that protects from spiritual darkness.

Diwali is celebrated with family gatherings, glittering clay lamps, festive fireworks, strings of electric lights, sharing of sweets, and worship to Lakshmi, the goddess of wealth and prosperity.

Diwali Traditions

Rangoli

Colorful patterns created on the floor using colored powders, flowers, or rice to welcome guests and bring good luck.

Diyas

Small oil lamps made of clay, lit to symbolize the victory of light over darkness, knowledge over ignorance.

Gift Exchange

Exchanging gifts with family, friends, and business associates to strengthen relationships and spread joy.

Feast

Preparing and sharing special festive meals and sweets with family and friends during the celebrations.

Puja

Prayers offered to Goddess Lakshmi for prosperity and Lord Ganesha for wisdom and good fortune.

Fireworks

Fireworks displays that light up the night sky, symbolizing the celebration of light conquering darkness.
